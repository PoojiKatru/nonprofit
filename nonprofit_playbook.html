Section 1

Nonprofit Strategy

🎯 Honest Target Audience Assessment

Elementary (K–5): Not your primary target. Not yet. Attention spans are too short for meaningful AI instruction. You'll spend more time managing behavior than teaching. The curriculum has to be so simplified it barely conveys anything real. Schools also have higher friction for external programs with younger kids. Save elementary for Year 2, simplified format only.

Middle school (6th–8th grade): This is your sweet spot. Old enough for critical thinking, young enough to be genuinely excited. They have social media, share content, influence their peers, and are at a formative age for building frameworks about technology. Start here. Build everything for this group first.

Mission Statement

"We equip middle schoolers with AI literacy and leadership skills — taught by high school students who are figuring it out alongside them."

What Makes You Different

You're teenagers

This is your superpower, not a weakness. Middle schoolers listen to high schoolers in a completely different way than they listen to adults. You're close enough to be relatable and far enough to be aspirational. Don't hide this — lead with it in every pitch.

Practical, not theoretical

Not "AI is the future." You teach how it actually works, where bias comes from, and how to think critically about technology they're already using every day. Students leave with real frameworks.

Leadership framing

Most AI programs for kids teach coding. You teach leadership in an AI world — who shapes it, who gets left out, and how young people can have a voice. This is genuinely differentiated.

TikTok-first community

You're building an audience that extends beyond any single workshop. Students who find you on TikTok keep learning. That's a compounding asset that workshop-only programs don't have.

3 Program Models

Model 1: The School Workshop (Primary)

60–90 minute in-school session. Free for the school. You bring everything. Target: 1–2 classrooms or an after-school program. This is your core product and social proof engine.

Pros: Photos, credibility, scale, repeat bookings. Barrier: Requires principal buy-in, 2–4 week lead time.

Model 2: The Community Session (Launch with This First)

90-minute event at a library, YMCA, or community center. Open to the public. Students sign up voluntarily. Much easier to book than schools — libraries almost always say yes. Use this to get your first event on the calendar.

Pros: Lowest barrier, great for getting started. Cons: You promote it yourself, lower guaranteed attendance.

Model 3: The Online Workshop (Scale Later)

60-minute Zoom session. Recruit from TikTok. Can reach students nationally. Run with 10–100 students. Free to operate, easy to record for social content.

Pros: National reach, scales fast, low cost. Cons: Less engaging than in-person, harder to photograph.

💡 Recommended Launch Order

Start with Model 2 (library) to lock your first confirmed date fast. Use those photos to pitch schools. Then add online workshops to scale. Don't try to run all three at once in the first 60 days.

Value Proposition for Schools

Lead with these exact points — in this order:

  • 100% free — no budget impact, no strings
  • Turnkey — you handle facilitation, materials, activities. Zero prep required from the teacher.
  • Student-led — kids engage differently with near-peers than adults
  • Digital literacy aligned — supports California's CA NGSS and digital literacy standards
  • One session, real impact — 75 minutes, measurable learning outcomes

Section 3

First Workshop:
Complete Build

🎓 "AI or Human? Understanding the Technology Shaping Your Future"

Duration: 75 minutes · Ages: 11–14 (6th–8th grade) · Max: 30 students · 3 hands-on activities · No lecture stretch over 8 minutes

Materials Needed

Free (you already have this)

  • Google Slides deck (you build this)
  • Laptop from school or yours
  • Projector (school or library provides)
  • Printed "AI or Human?" quiz cards — B&W, 1 per student
  • Pens or pencils

~$5 total (buy once, reuse)

  • 1 pack Post-it notes (~$3 at Dollar Tree)
  • Markers for group activities
  • Optional: small bag of candy for quiz winners (~$2)

Slide-by-Slide Outline + Full Script

1

Title Slide

"AI or Human? Understanding the Technology Shaping Your Future" · [Org Name] · [Date]

"Hey everyone — I'm [Name] and this is [Cofounder]. We're high school students from [school]. We built this program because nobody taught US about AI when we were your age. Today you're going to learn things your teachers might not know yet. Let's get into it."
2

Quick Poll — Show of Hands

Who has used ChatGPT? · Who uses Netflix, Spotify, or YouTube recommendations? · Who thinks AI will affect their future career?

"Look around the room. Almost everyone raised their hand for Netflix. That IS AI. You're already living inside an AI world — and today we're going to understand how it actually works."
3

What Is AI? Three points.

1) Software that learns from data and makes decisions · 2) Not magic — it's math · 3) Made by people, which means people's choices shape it

"Forget sci-fi robots. AI is software that gets better at a task the more data it gets. Netflix watches what you watch, how long, what you skip — and uses that to predict what you'll like next. That's it. No magic. Just patterns in data."
4

AI Is Already Everywhere (Interactive)

Show images: TikTok FYP · Google autocomplete · Siri · spam filters · face unlock · Grammarly. Ask students: "Which uses AI?"

"Tell me — which of these uses AI? [Take answers.] ALL of them. Every single one. You are already inside an AI world, every single day, whether you know it or not."
5
🎯 Activity 1 — 10 minutes

AI or Human? Quiz

Distribute printed quiz cards. Show 8 examples: an essay, a poem, a painting, a song recommendation, a translation, a legal brief summary, a news article, a drawing. Students write A (AI) or H (Human). Reveal answers. Award candy to top scorers.

"I'm going to show you 8 things. On your card, write AI or Human for each one. No Googling — trust your gut. After all 8, we'll reveal." [After reveal:] "What surprised you most? The AI ones are getting harder to spot. That's exactly why we need to understand this."
6

How Does AI Actually Learn?

Simple visual on slide: Data → Algorithm → Output → Feedback → Better Output. Compare to learning a video game.

"AI learns the same way you learn to play a video game. First time: you're bad. You fail. You learn what works. AI does the same — except it can fail and learn millions of times in seconds, using more data than any human could read in a lifetime."
7

AI Can Be Biased — And Here's Why That Matters

AI learns from human data → human data has human biases → real examples: facial recognition that works worse on darker skin tones, hiring AIs that penalize women's resumes, content algorithms that push extreme content for engagement.

"Here's the part nobody tells you. If you train an AI on biased data, you get a biased AI. And biased AI makes real decisions — who gets hired, who gets a loan, who gets flagged by security software. These are not hypotheticals. They already happened."
8
🎯 Activity 2 — 10 minutes

Bias Spotting

Groups of 3. Printed scenario cards. Scenario 1: Hiring AI trained on 10 years of resumes when tech was 80% male. Scenario 2: Medical AI trained primarily on data from white patients. Scenario 3: Content algorithm that maximizes watch time by showing increasingly extreme content. Each group answers: What's the bias? Who's hurt? What would you change?

"Groups of 3. Here's your scenario card. Answer three questions: What's the bias? Who does it hurt? What would you change to fix it? Five minutes — then we share." [Debrief: pull out the strongest idea from each group aloud.]
9

Leadership + AI

"AI doesn't lead. Humans do." — The people who understand AI will shape it. You don't need to code. You need to ask the right questions about who benefits, who's harmed, and who decides.

"You don't need to know how to code to be an AI leader. But you DO need to understand AI well enough to challenge the people making decisions about it — in government, in companies, in schools. That's leadership. And it could be you."
10
🎯 Activity 3 — 12 minutes

Design Your AI

Each student gets a Post-it. Three questions: 1) What problem in your school or community would you want AI to solve? 2) What data would it need to learn? 3) What could go wrong? Share 4–5 ideas aloud with the room.

"Now YOU think like an AI designer. 5 minutes — answer those 3 questions on your post-it. Then we'll share." [After sharing:] "These are real product ideas. Some of the most important AI tools of the next 10 years will come from people exactly like you."
11

What's Next + How to Stay Connected

Follow on TikTok. Join interest list. Tell your teacher you want more. Share with one person who wasn't here.

"You now know more about AI than most adults. That's not an exaggeration. The question is: what are you going to do with it? Follow us — we post AI tips, workshop highlights, and things schools aren't teaching you yet. Be the person in your friend group who actually understands this stuff."
12

Q&A + Group Photo

5–10 min for questions. Group photo at the end (with consent). Collect post-its for your content and records.

"Any questions? Anything you want to go deeper on? [Take 5–6 questions, keep energy up.] This was incredible — thank you for being so engaged. We're going to take a group photo to mark that you were part of our first ever [Org Name] workshop."

Conversion Strategy

  • Always attach the one-pager PDF — it answers their questions before they ask and signals you're professional
  • Offer specific dates in every follow-up — "Would June 12 or June 18 work?" converts far better than "let me know when you're free"
  • Make the first ask tiny — "just a quick email exchange" removes friction better than "can we schedule a call"
  • Lead with "free" in the subject line — principals are targeted by vendors constantly. Being free and student-led cuts through that
  • Keep the email short — principals read on their phones. If it's longer than 150 words, you'll lose them. The template above is calibrated for this.

Your Most Important Legal Step: Hack Club Bank

✅ Top Recommendation — Do This in Week 2

Hack Club Bank (hcb.hackclub.com) is free fiscal sponsorship specifically for student-led tech and education organizations. This is your legal structure until you're ready to file for 501(c)(3).

What you get: A legal entity to receive donations, a real FDIC-insured bank account, tax-deductible donation status under Hack Club's existing 501(c)(3), a debit card for expenses, and a transparent public financial ledger. All free, no upfront cost.

Apply at: hcb.hackclub.com/apply — they review applications and accept early-stage student orgs. Be honest that you're just launching. Timeline: 2–4 weeks.

How to Get an Adult Advisor

This is your most urgent need. Without it, you can't get into schools, sign verification letters, or use Hack Club Bank. Target: a teacher, parent, school counselor, or professional family friend.

💬 Script to Ask Someone to Be Your Adult Advisor
"[Name], I'm building something real with a friend — a student-led nonprofit that teaches middle schoolers about AI. We're already reaching out to schools and we're serious about making it happen. We need an adult advisor — someone who can sign volunteer verification letters and be available for occasional questions. It's probably 1–2 hours a month. You wouldn't be running anything, just supervising. Would you be willing to do that for us?"

501(c)(3): When to Apply

Apply in Year 2, after you have documented workshops, a track record of students reached, consistent volunteers, and ideally a board of 3–5 adults. By then your application is materially stronger and the $275–600 filing fee is more justifiable. Hack Club Bank's 501(c)(3) covers you completely in the meantime.

Simple Cofounder Agreement (No Lawyer Needed)

Write this as a Google Doc. Both sign electronically (or just type your names). Cover: roles and responsibilities, how decisions are made, what happens if one person leaves or goes inactive, how public credit is shared. This protects your friendship and prevents conflicts before they happen.

Section 9

Cofounder System

Person 1: Programs Director

Owns everything that happens in the real world
  • Workshop content, materials, activities
  • School and library partnerships
  • Volunteer training and day-of management
  • Impact tracking (students reached, hours logged)
  • Day-of execution for all workshops
Not responsible for: social media, cold outreach, finances

Person 2: Growth Director

Owns everything that gets people to show up
  • TikTok + Instagram (posting, replying, growing)
  • Cold outreach emails to schools + libraries
  • Website, one-pager, and materials updates
  • Sponsor and partner relationships
  • Financial tracking on Hack Club Bank
Not responsible for: workshop content, facilitation, volunteer training

Decision-Making System

  • Decisions within your own domain: Just make it. Tell the other person after. No vote needed. This is what prevents bottlenecks.
  • Cross-domain decisions or anything over $50: Both agree within 48 hours. State your case in a voice note or short text — clear, reasoned, brief.
  • Genuinely stuck? Low-stakes: flip a coin and commit. High-stakes: 15-minute call with your adult advisor for a third perspective.
  • The rule: "We'll decide together" cannot be used as an excuse to delay any decision more than 48 hours. Stalling kills nonprofit momentum faster than wrong decisions do.

Weekly Workflow

  • Every Sunday, 30 minutes: What got done? What's blocked? Top 3 priorities each person owns this week.
  • Shared Notion board: Three columns — To Do | In Progress | Done. Every task has one named owner, never "both of us."
  • Shared Google Calendar: All workshop dates, outreach deadlines, posting days, and the Sunday check-in itself
  • The rule: If a task doesn't have a single named owner, it doesn't get done. Assign everything.

How to Look Credible Fast
